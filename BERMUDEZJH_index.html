DEVELOPMENT OF FOLK DANCES IN THE WORLD

The universal language of the common people across history.

Folk dance is the oldest form of dance, predating written history. In ancient civilizations (Egypt, Greece, India), dance was a sacred ritual used to communicate with gods, ensure fertility, and prepare for war. It was communal, inclusive, and essential for social bonding.

As societies stratified, a divide emerged. The aristocracy developed refined "Court Dances" (like the Minuet), while the common people (the "Folk") continued to practice vigorous, earthy dances like the Maypole and Chain Dances. These were performed at village fairs, weddings, and seasonal festivals.

During the Industrial Revolution, nations feared losing their identity. This sparked "Romantic Nationalism," where scholars (like Cecil Sharp in England) traveled to villages to record steps and music. Folk dance became a symbol of national pride and political identity for countries like Poland (Mazurka), Hungary (Czardas), and Spain (Flamenco).

Today, folk dance is preserved through international organizations like CIOFF. It has evolved from a daily village activity into a performative art form ("Stage Folk Dance"), showcased in massive festivals to promote cross-cultural understanding and tourism.

PHILIPPINE FOLK DANCE

A rich tapestry of history, religion, and geography woven into movement.

Historical Evolution

Pre-colonial Era

The Roots of Animism

Before foreign influence, dance was intrinsically linked to the spiritual and daily life of the tribes. It was not merely entertainment but a necessity—a medium to appease the Gods, ask for rain, or thank the spirits for a bountiful harvest.

Mimicking Nature

Movements were often derived from the environment. Dances mimicked the flight of birds, the swaying of trees, and the movements of animals, serving as a way for the community to bond and tell stories of the hunt or war.

Spanish Colonial Era

East Meets West

The arrival of Spaniards in the 16th century brought Christianity and European culture. This created a unique fusion where indigenous steps were blended with the grace and etiquette of European court dances.

The Maria Clara Tradition

A new category of "Maria Clara" dances emerged, revolving around courtship, modesty, and Christianity. The clothing evolved into the Barong Tagalog and the Terno, and music incorporated Spanish guitars and rondallas.

American Era & Modernity

Commercialization & Jazz

In 1898, the American occupation introduced commercial entertainment, vaudeville, and new musical styles. While modernization threatened traditional practices, it also opened the Philippines to global culture.

Preservation & Nationalism

The most significant development came after World War II. As a reaction to rapid westernization, there was a strong movement to document and preserve ancient folk dances, transforming them into symbols of national pride and identity.

CLASSIFICATION OF PHILIPPINE FOLK DANCES

Dances are categorized by location, theme, and cultural influence, revealing the complexity of Filipino heritage.

National Folk Dances

Dances performed throughout the Philippines, representing the shared traditions and identity of the Filipino people.

Example: Cariñosa, Pandanggo sa Ilaw, Rigodon

Regional Folk Dances

Specific to certain provinces or localities, reflecting the unique traditions and way of life of the people from that region.

Example: Tinikling (Leyte), Singkil (Maranao), Maglalatik (Laguna)

Religious or Ritual Dances

Performed as part of spiritual ceremonies and beliefs, used to honor deities or ask for good fortune.

Example: Subli (Batangas), Dugso (Bukidnon)

Courtship Dances

Depict the romantic relationships between men and women, often involving playful gestures and symbolic actions that express affection.

Example: Kuratsa, Pantomina, Rogelia

Wedding Dances

Performed during marriage celebrations, symbolizing joy, unity, and the blessings of the community for the newlywed couple.

Example: Lulay, Soryano

Festival Dances

Joyful performances that celebrate community life, good harvests, and religious feasts. They feature vibrant costumes and energetic movements.

Example: Sayaw sa Bangko, Pahiyas Festival Dances

Occupational Dances

Portray the everyday work, livelihood, or industry of the people, showing how Filipinos celebrate their labor through rhythm and movement.

Example: Pangisda, Pagtatahip

War Dances

Dramatize the courage, strength, and heroism of warriors preparing for or celebrating victory in battle.

Example: Sagayan (Maranao), Igorot War Dance

Imitative Dances

Mimic the movements of animals, plants, or natural elements, showing the close connection between people and their environment.

Example: Itik-Itik (duck), Tinikling (tikling bird)

Famous Regional Dances

Tinikling

The national dance involving bamboo poles and agility.

Singkil

Royal Maranao dance telling the epic of Princess Gandingan.

Maglalatik

War dance using coconut shells as percussion instruments.

Pandanggo sa Ilaw

The dance of lights, balancing oil lamps on head and hands.

Cariñosa

Flirtatious courtship dance with fans and handkerchiefs.

Itik-Itik

Mimics the movements of a duck wading and swimming.

Our Performance — Tiklos

Tiklos is a traditional Visayan circle dance performed during celebrations and gatherings. It features graceful footwork and partner patterns performed in a linked circle, often accompanied by lively kundiman or folk tunes. The dance emphasizes community and cooperative movement, with a focus on rhythm and simple, elegant steps passed between participants.
